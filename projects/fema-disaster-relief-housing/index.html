FEMA Disaster-Related Housing Design

Structural analysis of disaster-relief housing designs based on location-specific environmental loading requriements

Undergraduate Researcher
MIT Digital Structures Lab
Supervisors: Courtney Stephen, Caitlin Mueller
Fall 2017 - Fall 2018

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) tasked researchers to rethink disaster response housing.

The research project clustered regions in the United States based on similarity in wind, snow, and seismic loading conditions. Loading maps for wind, snow, and seismic loads were overlayed in ArcGIS, which created an intersection of polygons. The polygons and their associated wind, snow, and seismic loading requriements, or "loading signature" were imported into MATLAB. The polygons were sorted by similarity in loading signatures using k-means clustering. A one-size-fits-all housing design would be overengineered for different parts of the country. Materials savings up to 60% could be saved if the regions of the US were clusterd into six different groups each with different loading requirements.

Using the Karamba3D strucutral analysis tool in Grasshopper beam and column elements of the deployable housing unit were sized parametrically for the maximum loading conditions of each cluster.
